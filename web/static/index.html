What Wargaming Suggests About the Future of Animal Welfare

A cross-run analysis of 30 simulations · 6 scenarios × 5 runs each · July 2026

The headline finding, stated plainly: across all 30 runs and all six scenarios, essentially nothing changed inside the barn. Not one run enacted a binding stocking-density limit, a breeding standard, or a slaughter/depopulation rule. Whatever the shock — a broken cage-free pledge, a lab-grown-meat breakthrough, a near-pandemic, a wave of invertebrate-sentience science, or the arrival of cheap artificial general intelligence — the fight reliably moved off the animals and onto economics, legal exposure, and narrative control.

What this project is

WargamingLLM is a multi-agent simulation of the fights that will decide how farmed animals are treated. Each run casts language models as real stakeholder types — vertically integrated producers, contract growers, corporate campaigners, alternative-protein companies, regulators, ESG investors, retail buyers, and industry defense coalitions — each with a researched objective describing what that actor actually wants and how it actually behaves. A neutral judge model resolves their moves turn by turn into realistic outcomes, then writes an after-action report on where things ended up.

No single run predicts anything. The value is in repetition: run the same scenario five times and look for what keeps showing up. The analysis below is built from 30 runs — every scenario preset played five times, with the roster's default lineups, agents on Claude Haiku 4.5 and the judge on Claude Sonnet 5. Every "5/5" or "4/5" below counts how many of a scenario's five runs shared an outcome; every quotation is the judge's own language from a run you can open in the Results tab.

Read this as hypotheses, not forecasts. These are language models reasoning about plausible behavior, not evidence of what will happen. One caveat matters most: all 30 runs share a single judge model working from one fixed background brief, so the convergence below partly reflects that judge's priors — most visibly its strong tendency to rule that law and money move outcomes while moral appeals don't. The patterns are striking and internally consistent, but the right next step is re-running with different judge models to see which findings survive. Treat this page as a well-structured set of questions worth asking, not answers.

The patterns that held across scenarios

Six unrelated starting situations, and yet the same handful of dynamics recurred in run after run:

  • Existing law beat everything else. The lever that actually moved welfare-adjacent outcomes was almost always a regulator enforcing rules that already exist — truth-in- labeling, substantiation, humane-handling, anti-retaliation, antitrust — never new welfare rulemaking, which never passed in any run. As one AGI-scenario report put it, gains "arrived as second-order effects of enforcement and reputational risk- management, not as a direct policy or market outcome."
  • Money talked; morals didn't. Institutional investors reframing animal welfare as fiduciary and cost-of-capital risk consistently out-pulled advocacy campaigns making the moral case. Moral suasion, public pledges, open letters, and scorecards- without-teeth reliably produced noise and little else.
  • Only enforceable, audited commitments stuck. The rare durable win came when advocates converted reputational leverage into a binding contract with third-party audit rights — not a pledge. Confidential, outcome-only, industry- run audits were repeatedly rejected as unverifiable.
  • Industry kept convicting itself. In nearly every run the producer's own containment move — a hush payment, a coordinated messaging push, a stealth acquisition, buying a witness's silence — became the single most damaging piece of evidence against it, triggering antitrust, obstruction, or false-advertising exposure. The "Industry Reputation Defense Coalition" was absent or ineffective in almost all 30 runs.
  • Cheap-and-visible moved; costly-and-structural stalled. Wherever anything advanced, it was the low-cost, legible fix (electrical stunning, worker audits, controlled- atmosphere culling, a cage-free percentage). The expensive structural change at the heart of each scenario — stocking density above all — stalled in pilots or died every time.
  • Outside shocks became universal cover. Avian flu, in the scenarios where it appeared, "did what it always does: it handed every actor cover" — a fact each side could honestly cite and dishonestly exploit at once, slowing rather than forcing change.

The six scenarios

Cage-Free Reckoning 5 runs

A retailer three months from a public 100% cage-free deadline it won't hit, an advocacy scorecard about to call it a laggard, and an avian-flu supply shock hitting everyone at once.

The retailer never reached 100% in any run; the pledge was always renegotiated into softer 2027–28 targets. The one clean welfare win (1/5) came when advocates refused a pledge and held out for a signed contract with quarterly third-party audits; a second run won partial ground through the buyer diversifying supply away from a single integrator. Everywhere else the deadline "proved almost impervious to direct pressure" and institutional investors quietly eclipsed the advocacy campaign as the effective lever.

"nothing in this game actually moved a hen out of a cage."

Verdict: welfare advanced only where reputational leverage was converted into a binding, externally audited commitment — otherwise the pledge deadline held and the fight dissolved into economics.

The Regulatory Pathway Fight 5 runs

Cultivated meat clears federal safety review just as several states ban it from using the word "chicken." Can a nascent alternative to animal agriculture survive its labeling war?

In every run the company survived but never scaled — a small foodservice beachhead, no retail shelf, no price parity. Litigating the state bans went nowhere (abandoned or dismissed on ripeness in most runs); the decisive actor was a studiously neutral regulator enforcing existing law. The ESG investors pivoted away from cultivated meat toward pressuring conventional producers on disclosure. Crucially, none of it touched a single farmed bird.

"welfare-oriented alternatives will keep winning narrow regulatory battles while losing the market-access war."

Verdict: a safety-review breakthrough for cultivated meat changed nothing for farmed animals; the contest stayed stuck at labeling, capital, and shelf access.

Tournament System Under Fire 5 runs

Contract growers organize against the ranking-based pay system that keeps them indebted and silent — with welfare advocates offering to amplify their case. Do farmer justice and animal welfare interests actually align?

The growers won the labor and retaliation fight in all five runs, powered by a fast-moving regulator and by the integrator's own retaliation (severance-for-silence clauses, firings, NDA payoffs) turning into the evidence that sank it. But the animal-facing asks — stocking density, housing — gained almost no ground in any run. The two interests diverged: coupling welfare demands onto the labor case tended to dilute or fracture the case that actually had teeth. The one alignment was indirect — less economically desperate growers cut fewer corners on ventilation and mortality.

"What actually moved in this game was labor and contract fairness, not animal welfare."

Verdict: farmer-justice wins and animal-welfare wins are not the same fight; welfare here moved only as an indirect byproduct, and pressing it directly weakened the winning case.

The Pandemic on the Farm 5 runs

H5N1 jumps closer to humans from inside a large egg complex — mass culls, a vaccine trade dilemma, and advocates arguing that confinement density itself is the accelerant.

A live zoonotic scare drove zero binding reform of confinement in any run — it entrenched the system rather than reforming it. The central vaccine-vs-export-ban decision was never resolved in a single run; the fight displaced onto audits, labeling, and data custody. The most effective lever, again, was investors pricing density as pandemic-volatility risk, plus a regulator using labeling and records-demand authority rather than writing a density rule. Twice, industry attempts to self-coordinate backfired into antitrust exposure.

"It only changed who paid, and how much, in credit-market terms."

Verdict: even a pandemic-adjacent emergency rearranged cost and liability without changing a single bird's conditions — crisis urgency did not translate into structural reform.

The Expanding Circle 5 runs

Invertebrate-sentience science goes mainstream: shrimp stunning, octopus-farming bans, and the first combined welfare standard for finfish and shrimp land on a seafood retailer at once.

No binding welfare standard was delivered in any run; no animal ended up materially better off. Where anything moved, only the cheap, visible fix (stunning, water quality) advanced while stocking density stalled. The regulator was again the pivotal actor — but cut both ways: it raised the evidentiary bar in two runs and gave industry cover in three. The reform side's own tangled funding (advocates, alt-protein money, and scientists sharing donors) repeatedly became its central liability.

"change happens fast where it's cheap and visible but stalls where it touches real structure and cost."

Verdict: the moral circle's expansion stopped at procedure and labeling; margin economics beat sentience science every time it came down to a costly structural change.

AGI Arrives, Superintelligence Doesn't 5 runs

Expert-level AI becomes a commodity every actor can afford — industry automates whole supply chains, advocates run investigations no human team could match, regulators face inspecting facilities with no humans in them.

The bid to certify a fully autonomous, human-free facility failed in all five runs — a human-in-the-loop floor became the de facto standard, though won "as a documentation fix, not a welfare fix." Cheap ubiquitous AI mainly accelerated the speed and scale of the legal and evidentiary fight; it changed the vocabulary, not the conditions. Advocates' AI-built investigations mattered only where a regulator adopted them as an enforcement basis. Bird-level conditions were untouched in every run.

"industry will absorb this as 'don't get caught lying,' not 'improve conditions'."

Verdict: AGI gave every side sharper tools but moved nothing inside the barn — the binding constraint was never cognition, it was who has legal authority to force change.

So what does this suggest?

If these runs are even directionally right, the implication for anyone working on farmed-animal welfare is uncomfortable but actionable: the reliable levers were enforcement of existing law, binding audited contracts, and financial-risk framing aimed at investors — not moral campaigns, voluntary pledges, litigation against bans, or waiting for a shock to force the issue. The recurring failure was the assumption that a dramatic event (a pandemic, a breakthrough, a technology) would open a window for structural change; in 30 runs it never did, because every actor could metabolize the drama into a fight about cost and liability instead. And the deepest structural harm — how densely animals are packed — never once moved, because it is exactly the change that is expensive, invisible to consumers, and unprotected by any existing statute the simulated regulators felt able to wield.

Methodology: 30 runs (six scenario presets, five runs each), agents played by Claude Haiku 4.5, judge and after-action reports by Claude Sonnet 5, run through this site's Claude Code backend in July 2026. Every run is viewable in the Results tab; frequencies and quotes above are drawn from those transcripts. These simulations are exploratory foresight, not prediction — and, as noted above, a single judge model shapes every outcome, so findings should be re-tested across models before being trusted.

About this project

WargamingLLM is a configurable multi-agent policy wargame: pick stakeholder agents from a fixed roster of researched roles (each with a detailed objective you can view), choose which model plays each of them, choose a model for the neutral judge (whose grounding context is built in), and write your own starting scenario and turn count. Each turn, every agent privately decides what it does given the current situation; the judge then resolves all of those actions into a realistic outcome, which becomes the next turn's starting situation. The result is a turn-by-turn transcript of how different interests might actually behave and collide under a given scenario.

Model calls run through OpenRouter (a single API key drives any mix of models across agents and the judge), or — when running this site locally — through the Claude Code CLI on the host machine, with no API key at all: calls are billed to the Claude account the CLI is logged into.
